Overview

What is Biodiversity Loss?

Biodiversity refers to the incredible variety of life on Earth — from the tiniest microbe to the largest whale. Biodiversity loss occurs when species disappear due to human activities such as deforestation, climate change, pollution, and habitat destruction.

Healthy ecosystems provide food, medicine, clean water, and climate regulation. When biodiversity declines, entire ecosystems weaken — and ultimately, so do the foundations of human survival and prosperity.

Forest Resources

The word forest comes from the Latin word "foris", which means "outside." Originally, it referred to land outside the village that was not cultivated or inhabited, with natural plants like trees and grasses. According to Allen and Shorpe, a forest is a large area with many trees and other living organisms that use air, water, and minerals to grow and reproduce.

Depletion of Forest Resources

People have used forests since ancient times. Before, the use of forest resources was limited and balanced with natural growth. Today, forests are being used on a large scale for industrial and personal needs. Because of this, forests are being depleted faster than they can recover.

Main Reasons for Large-Scale Depletion

🌾 Expansion of agriculture
🏘️ Urbanization & settlements
🪓 Commercial logging
🔥 Forest fires

Deforestation

Deforestation means reckless or large-scale felling or cutting of trees by man for commercial and other purposes. The FAO defines it as "change of forest with depletion of tree crown cover more than 90%."

Even though 75% of the Earth is covered by water, only about 3% is fresh water, and most of it is not easy to access.

The Hydrologic Cycle

The hydrologic cycle is a continuous process in which water moves through various phases and locations on earth.

💨 Evaporation

Water changes from liquid to vapor due to sun's heat in oceans, rivers, and lakes

🌿 Transpiration

Plants release water vapor into the atmosphere

☁️ Condensation

Water vapor turns into tiny droplets, forming clouds

🌧️ Precipitation

Water falls as rain, snow, or ice

💧 Infiltration

Water seeps into the ground

🏞️ Surface Runoff

Water flows into rivers, lakes, or oceans

🌊 Groundwater Flow

Water moves beneath the surface

Global Climate Change & Biodiversity

Climate change is one of the most powerful drivers of biodiversity loss today. Rising temperatures, shifting rainfall patterns, and extreme weather events are transforming habitats faster than most species can adapt — pushing countless life forms toward extinction.

How Climate Change Harms Biodiversity

🐋

Ocean Warming & Acidification

Warmer, more acidic oceans bleach coral reefs and disrupt marine food webs, threatening millions of aquatic species.

🏔️

Melting Polar Ice

The rapid loss of Arctic and Antarctic ice destroys habitats for polar bears, penguins, and countless other species.

🌧️

Shifting Seasons

Altered rainfall and seasonal patterns break migration, breeding, and food availability timing for wildlife.

🔥

Extreme Wildfires

Climate-driven wildfires destroy vast forest ecosystems, displacing and killing species at unprecedented scales.


Addressing climate change is inseparable from protecting biodiversity. Forests, wetlands, and oceans act as carbon sinks — when we protect nature, we also slow the pace of climate change itself.

What is Acid Deposition?

Acid deposition refers to the process where sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) emitted from industrial activities, vehicles, and power plants react with water, oxygen, and other chemicals in the atmosphere to form acidic compounds. These compounds fall to Earth as:
• Wet deposition: Acid rain, fog, and snow
• Dry deposition: Acidic gases and particles

Connection to Global Warming

🏭

Common Sources

Both acid deposition and global warming stem primarily from burning fossil fuels. Coal-fired power plants and vehicles emit CO₂ (causing warming) alongside SO₂ and NOₓ (causing acid rain).

🌡️

Amplified Effects

Warmer temperatures accelerate chemical reactions that form acid rain. Climate change also alters weather patterns, concentrating acid deposition in vulnerable ecosystems.

🌲

Ecosystem Stress

Forests weakened by acid rain become more susceptible to drought, pests, and wildfires — all intensified by global warming, creating a vicious cycle of degradation.

Devastating Impacts on Biodiversity

🌊

Aquatic Ecosystems

Acid rain leaches aluminum from soil into water bodies, killing fish, amphibians, and aquatic insects. Over 70% of lakes in some regions show acidification damage.

🌳

Forests & Soil

Acid deposition strips soil of essential nutrients like calcium and magnesium, stunting tree growth and making forests vulnerable to disease and extreme weather.

🏛️

Infrastructure

Acidic compounds accelerate the erosion of buildings, monuments, and cultural heritage sites, causing billions in damage annually.

👥

Human Health

Fine sulfate and nitrate particles from acid deposition contribute to respiratory diseases like asthma and bronchitis, especially in urban areas.

Solutions & Mitigation Strategies

⚡ Transition to renewable energy
🚗 Promote electric vehicles
🏭 Install scrubbers in factories
🌱 Reforest damaged areas
⚖️ Strengthen emissions regulations
🧪 Liming acidified lakes/soils
📊 Monitor air quality
🌍 International cooperation

Success stories show hope: Through the Clean Air Act and similar legislation, SO₂ emissions have decreased by 90% since 1990 in some regions, demonstrating that coordinated action can reverse environmental damage.
